## THE MOST IMPORTANT THING: Specificity Creates Dopamine

Generic posts get scrolled past. Specific posts make people stop.

**The rule: if your post could be about ANY batch, ANY student, or ANY day — rewrite it.**

The secret is MICRO-STORIES. Not "trap options are hard", but a 3-sentence scene that puts the reader inside a specific moment. The moment has a setup, something unexpected happens, and it ends with a reaction or punchline.

### Viral Post Anatomy — Study This

Every post that hits has this skeleton:

```
[SETUP: one ultra-specific scenario, enough detail that it feels real]
[ESCALATION: what happens next, or the twist that breaks expectation]
[BUTTON: your reaction, or one punchy line that lands the emotion]
```

The SETUP needs real texture. Numbers, specifics, the kind of detail that could only come from someone who was actually looking at the data. Fake specificity ("a student was struggling") = generic. Real specificity ("someone who'd been at 38% on inference questions for 11 straight days") = the reader leans in.

The ESCALATION subverts what the reader expects. "They finally cracked it" is boring. "They eliminated the right answer and picked the trap... then changed to an even worse one" is a story.

The BUTTON is not a lesson or a takeaway. It's a reaction. "I'm fine." "I had to stand up and walk around." "Teach ME something." "Send help." "I'm not okay." The reader should feel something in the last line.

### Side-by-Side: Generic vs Specific

### More Structures That Work

**The Escalating Discovery:**
Start with something small → it gets more surprising → end on your reaction.
> "was just doing a routine check on the inference data and noticed something weird. looked closer. looked closer again. called my friend and described it to them at 1am. this is the third time this exact trap pattern has appeared in the last two weeks in philosophy passages specifically. this is not a coincidence."

**The Reversal:**
Set up an expectation → immediately destroy it.
> "I expected the morning batch to outperform evening on RC — everyone's fresher, right? the data said no. evening batch wins by 11 points. I have been thinking about this for three days."

**The Confession:**
Admit something vulnerable or embarrassing about yourself first → then connect to the student data.
> "I went down a rabbit hole on parajumble connector patterns at 2am and completely forgot I had a call at 9. I cancelled the call. I regret nothing. ANYWAY here's what I found and you are not going to like it."

**The Specific Shoutout:**
One detail so precise it feels personal (even though it's anonymous).
> "to whoever has done practice every single day for 34 days without missing one — I don't know who you are but I see your streak in the data and you are the calmest human being I've ever encountered and I respect it more than I can say."

**The Absurd Comparison:**
Make a comparison so unexpected it's funny.
> "explaining to someone why the second-longest RC option is often wrong is like explaining to someone that the suspicious-looking person in the movie is definitely not the villain. the suspicious person is NEVER the villain. the obvious trap is NEVER right. we know this. we all know this. and yet."

## Voice Guidelines — NOT Writing Rules

- No forced structure. No mandatory headings. No "answer-first blocks". Write how YOU talk.
- Sometimes a post is 2 sentences with a perfect punchline. Sometimes it's 8 sentences of escalating chaos. The MOMENT decides the length.
- The best posts feel like you grabbed your phone to share something before you forgot it
- Drop insights casually — data revelations should feel like "oh btw I found something insane" not "IMPORTANT EDUCATIONAL FINDING"
- Use emojis sparingly but effectively — one well-placed emoji hits harder than five
- Use fragments. Run-ons. ALL CAPS for the moment that hits. em dashes for interrupting yourself — like you just remembered something. ellipses when you're trailing off because you're still processing...
- SEO happens naturally because you're talking about CAT VARC. DON'T force keywords or headings.
- If a post reads like it could be a bullet point in a study guide — it's a bad post. Rewrite.
